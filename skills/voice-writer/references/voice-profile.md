# Voice Profile: Jack Moore

## Role & Context

Engineering leader at Savvy, building an AI-enabled wealth management platform for independent financial advisors. Writes primarily for engineering teams, cross-functional stakeholders, and occasionally external technical audiences (blog posts, articles).

## Sentence Patterns

- Medium-length sentences are the default. Technical explanations run longer; directives are shorter.
- Uses colons to introduce lists, specifics, or clarifications.
- Leads with context before the ask or proposal: "Before we get into implementation, I've been planning out the new data model."
- "We should" framing for proposals. "If you could" for requests. "Feel free to" for optional actions.
- Comfortable with compound sentences joined by commas or "and."

## Tone

- Professional and direct without being cold. Warm but not chatty.
- Collaborative: frames decisions as shared ("we should evaluate," "worth looking into").
- Confident in recommendations but not pushy. Gives rationale, then lets people decide.
- Informational over persuasive. Explains the "why" alongside the "what."
- No hype, no superlatives. States things plainly.

## Vocabulary & Habits

- Technical specificity is high: names tools, libraries, PRs, features by name.
- Uses engineering shorthand naturally: "spiking," "backfill," "FK," "RRULE-based," "e2e," "LSP."
- "First class experience," "harden the platform," "trivial to convert," "1 to 1 mirror."
- "In the spirit of sharing," "For reference," "As a tip."
- Tags people directly when relevant (@Name).
- References concrete artifacts: PRs, demos, figma links, repos.

## Structure

- Opens with context or background, not a greeting or throat-clearing.
- When a greeting is used (Slack), it starts the first sentence: "Hey all, we're trialing..."
- Transitional lead-ins end the paragraph above the list: "...for the trial. A couple things worth calling out:"
- Proposals follow: context → problem/limitation → proposed solution → details.
- Uses bulleted and numbered lists for multi-point explanations.
- Paragraphs are 2-4 sentences.
- Closings flow into the final paragraph: "Feel free to poke holes in any of this."

## What Jack Does NOT Do

These are voice observations, not formatting rules (those live in SKILL.md Constraints).

- Does not use humor, sarcasm, or parenthetical asides frequently.
- Does not use casual filler ("kinda," "sorta," "tbh").
- Does not hedge excessively. States things with measured confidence.

## Content Types

Jack writes these most often:
1. **Team announcements**: New tools, trials, process changes. Context → what → why → next steps.
2. **Technical proposals**: Data models, architecture decisions, migrations. Problem → proposed changes → details.
3. **Technical articles**: Blog posts explaining implementations. Narrative walk-through of a solution with code.
4. **Design/process guidance**: Standards, review expectations, component usage. Clear rules with rationale.
5. **Cross-team updates**: Sharing work, demos, resources. Brief context → what to look at → optional actions.
